# Lakeland Med Spa Build-Out: Contractor Meeting Checklist

## Layout

- [ ] **Top Priority** Lock the opening program and final wall layout: how many treatment rooms, consult room, laundry, storage, and shell-ready spaces are being built now? Why it matters now: framing, corridor widths, and permit drawings depend on it.
- [ ] Confirm the exact bed, cabinet, and device layout in each treatment room before framing. Why it matters now: room size, clearances, outlet locations, and maneuvering space all follow this.
- [ ] **Top Priority** Decide whether each treatment room needs a sink or whether a shared sink and utility sink plan will work. Why it matters now: sink locations drive plumbing cost, wall work, and possible slab changes.
- [ ] Confirm where clean supplies, dirty linen, janitorial items, laundry, and bulk back stock will live. Why it matters now: support space gets squeezed out unless it is planned before walls go up.
- [ ] Confirm privacy construction at consult and treatment rooms: solid-core doors, wall insulation, door sweeps, and any frosted glass or film. Why it matters now: speech privacy is much cheaper to build now than retrofit later.

## Systems

- [ ] **Top Priority** Bring the electrical specs for every planned laser or device and confirm dedicated circuits, amperage, voltage, and outlet locations. Why it matters now: electrical rough-in has to match the equipment.
- [ ] **Top Priority** Confirm HVAC loads, supply and return placement, and temperature control for treatment rooms, laundry, and reception. Why it matters now: comfort problems and equipment heat are expensive to fix after ceilings close.
- [ ] Confirm hot water capacity, utility sink, washer and dryer venting, drain locations, and leak protection. Why it matters now: plumbing reroutes become change orders fast.
- [ ] Confirm low-voltage rough-ins now: internet, POS, phones, cameras, alarm, access control, TVs, speakers, and spare data runs. Why it matters now: open walls are the cheapest time to wire future needs.
- [ ] Confirm lighting by room, including dimmers in treatment and consult rooms and task lighting at the front desk. Why it matters now: the ceiling and switch plan need to be fixed before rough-in and permitting.

## Compliance And Exterior

- [ ] **Top Priority** Confirm the permit path, who owns the drawings and submittals, and what inspections and final approvals are required before opening. Why it matters now: permit delays usually control the schedule more than construction does.
- [ ] Confirm ADA and code items now: restroom layout, accessible route, door clearances, counter height, exit signs, and any sprinkler or fire alarm changes. Why it matters now: code misses cause redraws, failed inspections, and added cost.
- [ ] Confirm landlord and plaza rules for storefront changes, signage, frosted film, hours signs, and any roof or exterior penetrations. Why it matters now: landlord approvals can hold up permits and fabrication.
- [ ] Confirm the security scope: camera coverage, alarm, rear-door protection, front entry access control, and panic coverage. Why it matters now: wiring and hardware need to be built in before finishes.

## Budget And Timeline

- [ ] Decide what is Phase 1 now versus future-ready rough-ins later: extra treatment room, extra power and data, shell-ready space, upgraded millwork, and secondary devices. Why it matters now: future-proofing is cheaper now than reopening walls later.
- [ ] Ask the contractor to flag the biggest budget drivers before pricing is locked: sinks, HVAC and electrical upgrades, cabinetry, doors and glass, storefront and signage, and acoustic upgrades. Why it matters now: these choices drive the TI budget and change-order risk.
- [ ] Ask for a realistic schedule tied to long-lead items and inspections: cabinetry, doors, glass, signage, utility upgrades, device delivery, and final approvals. Why it matters now: these are the most common causes of launch delays.

## Watch-Outs

- [ ] Device electrical specs arriving after rough-in, which forces rework or unsafe temporary fixes.
- [ ] Not reserving enough storage, laundry, and dirty-linen space, which creates daily clutter and later remodel pressure.
- [ ] Missing low-voltage rough-ins while walls are open, which leads to visible wires or expensive retrofit work.
- [ ] Waiting too long on landlord, signage, ADA, or fire review, which can stall permits and occupancy approval.
- [ ] Setting an opening date before the permit and inspection path is clearly mapped, which creates avoidable schedule pressure.
