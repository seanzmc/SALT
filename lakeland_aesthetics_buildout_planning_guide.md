# **Quick-Start Snapshot**

**☐ Recommended room program:** 1 reception/waiting area, 1 consultation room, 4 treatment rooms, 1 provider workroom/charting area, 1 laundry/utility/storage room, 1 staff break nook, 1 ADA-accessible restroom, 1 back-of-house general storage area.

**☐ Lean opening version:** Front desk \+ waiting, consultation room, 2 treatment rooms, shared provider/workroom, laundry/storage, restroom; leave shell-ready capacity for 1-2 future treatment rooms.

**☐ Most common budget pressure points:** Tenant improvements, electrical/HVAC upgrades, cabinetry, laser devices, treatment beds, software subscriptions, signage, inspections, initial supplies, and working capital.

**☐ Most common timeline risks:** Permit review, change orders, utility upgrades, long-lead cabinetry/doors/glass, device delivery timing, inspection scheduling, staff hiring, training, and documentation setup.

# **1\. Space Planning & Layout**

## **Reception and Waiting Area**

**☐ Check-in visibility:** Position the front desk with a clean line of sight to the entry, waiting area, and corridor entrance to treatment rooms.

**☐ Traffic flow:** Separate client-facing circulation from back-of-house clutter. Clients should not see storage overflow, dirty linen, or open staff prep areas.

**☐ Retail opportunity:** Reserve wall or millwork space for curated retail without overwhelming the room. Keep display lighting flattering and easy to re-merchandise.

**☐ Comfort and acoustics:** Use upholstery and soft surfaces to reduce echo. Conversations at the front desk should not carry deep into the waiting area.

**☐ Power and data:** Plan desk outlets, data drops, receipt printer location, phone charging, POS equipment, camera views, and concealed cable routing.

## **Consultation Room**

**☐ Purpose:** Use for consultations, treatment plan discussions, consent review, photography, financing, and occasional skin analysis or patch testing if appropriate.

**☐ Privacy:** Make this room visually and acoustically private. It often hosts pricing, medical history, and sensitive before-and-after review.

**☐ Furnishings:** Desk or consult table, 2-3 guest chairs, provider chair, monitor or tablet stand, lockable storage, mirror, and strong task lighting.

## **Treatment Rooms**

**☐ Count strategy:** A practical 1,600 sq ft clinic can often support 3 fully active treatment rooms plus a 4th flex room for overflow, future device use, or injectable/facial expansion if legally appropriate.

**☐ Room shape:** Rectangular rooms with a clear side or foot approach to the treatment bed work better than narrow rooms that force staff to pivot around carts.

**☐ Minimum functional clearances:** Preserve circulation around the bed, device parking space, stool movement, and safe cable routing from wall outlets to laser device position.

**☐ Sink strategy:** Determine early whether sinks are needed in each room or if a shared sink strategy is legally and operationally acceptable. This affects plumbing cost materially.

**☐ Consistency:** Make room layouts as standardized as possible so staff can move between rooms without hunting for supplies or switching workflow patterns.

## **Provider Workroom, Break Area, Restroom, Storage, and Laundry**

**☐ Provider workroom:** Use as the operations nerve center for charting, label printing, charging devices, backup stock, forms, and locked storage.

**☐ Break nook:** Even a small staff area improves morale and keeps food and drink out of treatment zones. Include undercounter fridge, microwave, water access, and personal storage if feasible.

**☐ Restroom:** Verify ADA accessibility, finish durability, touchless accessories where desired, and cleaning supply storage. Restroom location should not force clients through back-of-house.

**☐ Storage zones:** Plan separate clean supply, retail overstock, janitorial, and equipment accessory storage rather than one chaotic closet.

**☐ Laundry/utility:** Provide space for washer, dryer, folding surface, detergent storage, dirty/clean linen separation, hampers, and ventilation.

## **HIPAA and Privacy Considerations in the Layout**

**☐ Speech privacy:** Avoid check-in and consult setups where other clients can overhear health history, treatment plans, payment details, or before-and-after discussions.

**☐ Screen privacy:** Position monitors and tablets so protected information is not visible from public seating or corridors.

**☐ Document control:** Design lockable storage for forms, retail receipts containing PHI if applicable, and shredding/recycling stations.

**☐ Photography privacy:** Have a consistent private location for before-and-after photos with neutral backdrop, controlled lighting, and clear consent workflow.

**☐ Sound isolation:** Use insulated walls, solid-core doors where practical, and door bottoms/sweeps if speech transfer becomes a concern.

# **2\. Construction & Build-Out**

## **Core Building Work**

**☐ Framing and drywall:** Confirm wall locations with furniture and equipment plans before framing. Add backing in walls for mounted shelves, TVs, dispensers, mirrors, and future accessories.

**☐ Doors:** Use durable commercial doors and hardware. Consider solid-core doors for treatment and consultation rooms to improve privacy.

**☐ Insulation:** Include acoustic insulation in treatment room and consultation room partitions, especially walls shared with waiting or neighboring tenants.

**☐ Flooring:** Prioritize non-porous, durable, easy-clean surfaces with slip resistance and a polished healthcare-adjacent appearance. Avoid flooring that traps dust or is difficult to sanitize.

**☐ Paint and wall finishes:** Choose washable low-sheen commercial coatings. Matte can look upscale but may scuff faster in corridors and treatment rooms.

**☐ Ceilings:** Coordinate ceiling system with lighting, speakers, diffusers, sprinklers, cameras, and access panels. Do not force later rework because devices were not coordinated.

## **Plumbing and Water**

**☐ Sink planning:** Map every handwashing and utility need before drawings are finalized. Late sink additions are expensive.

**☐ Hot water:** Confirm water heater capacity and recovery rate for handwashing, restroom, laundry, and cleaning demands.

**☐ Drain strategy:** Utility sink, restroom, washer box, and any wet-treatment functions should be planned together to minimize costly trenching or rerouting.

**☐ Leak protection:** Use pans, shutoffs, and sensible placement for washer/dryer and under-sink equipment where feasible.

## **Electrical, Data, and Low Voltage**

**☐ Dedicated circuits:** Each major device may require a dedicated circuit or specific amperage/voltage. Obtain electrical specs before final electrical rough-in.

**☐ Outlet placement:** Place standard and dedicated outlets intentionally around treatment bed positions, front desk, consult room, laundry, break area, TVs, printers, and signage.

**☐ Surge and power protection:** Use surge protection, power conditioning where indicated by manufacturer, and battery backup for critical networking and front-desk systems.

**☐ IT/network cabling:** Hardwire front desk, security, phones, printers, and any stationary work areas. Pull extra data runs while walls are open.

**☐ Access control wiring:** Pre-wire entry, rear door, alarm panels, cameras, and door strikes/readers even if full system activation happens later.

## **HVAC, Ventilation, and Lighting**

**☐ Thermal comfort:** Treatment rooms should be comfortable for lightly clothed clients and moving providers. Uneven temperatures become a daily complaint source.

**☐ Ventilation:** Review air changes, return placement, and odor control where topical products, cleaning agents, or laundry heat create stuffiness.

**☐ Humidity and lint:** Laundry areas need sensible ventilation and housekeeping to prevent heat, moisture, and lint accumulation.

**☐ Lighting by zone:** Reception should feel flattering and branded; consult rooms need balanced, color-accurate light; treatment rooms need controllable bright task light plus calming ambient options; back-of-house needs functional brightness.

**☐ Controls:** Install dimmers, switch legs, or scene control where appropriate rather than making every room a single all-or-nothing lighting condition.

## **Acoustics, Safety, Exterior, and ADA**

**☐ Soundproofing:** Prioritize acoustic privacy at consult and treatment rooms, especially if sharing demising walls or if plaza noise is high.

**☐ Signage and storefront:** Coordinate landlord rules, municipal sign permits, hours signage, suite numbering, frosted film, and visibility from parking.

**☐ Security:** Alarm system, glass-break sensors if relevant, monitored cameras, panic strategy for staff, and controlled after-hours access should be planned before finish-out is complete.

**☐ Fire safety:** Coordinate extinguisher locations, exit signage, occupant load, sprinkler alterations if any, and fire alarm modifications with licensed professionals and the AHJ.

**☐ ADA/accessibility:** Verify routes, door clearances, restroom layout, thresholds, turning radii, counter heights, and signage requirements with architect/GC.

# **4\. Laser Hair Removal & Aesthetic Equipment**

## **Core Device Categories**

**☐ Primary hair-removal platform:** Select a platform suitable for your intended treatment volume, target skin types, treatment speed goals, service menu, staffing model, and budget.

**☐ Secondary treatment capability:** Decide whether opening phase includes only hair removal or also adjacent aesthetics services such as photofacial, pigment, vascular, skin rejuvenation, or skin tightening where lawful and clinically appropriate.

**☐ Device room matching:** Assign each major device to a primary room and confirm electrical, cooling, storage, and maneuvering requirements there.

## **Accessories, Comfort, and Safety**

**☐ Handpieces and consumables:** Extra tips, distance gauges, treatment windows, gel if required, disposables, foot pedals, cords, and manufacturer-recommended replacement parts.

**☐ Cooling and comfort:** Integrated cooling, external cooling device, cooling packs, fans, stress balls, pillows, blankets, and post-treatment soothing supplies.

**☐ Eye protection:** Patient and provider eye protection matched to each device or wavelength as required by the manufacturer and safety guidance.

**☐ Photography/documentation:** Dedicated camera or high-quality clinic tablet/phone policy, neutral backdrop, ring light or panel light, tripod, measurement guides, and standardized photo angles.

## **Ownership, Service, and Uptime Planning**

**☐ Warranty/service contract:** Compare included warranty, loaner policy, response time, preventive maintenance visits, software updates, and consumable lock-in.

**☐ Calibration/maintenance planning:** Maintain logs for service calls, preventive maintenance, repairs, calibration/inspection where applicable, and downtime tracking.

**☐ Backup workflow:** Have a plan for schedule disruption if a device is down: service contacts, treatment rescheduling SOP, and client communication templates.

**☐ Training and credentials:** Document manufacturer training, protocols, medical oversight requirements, contraindication checklists, and competency sign-offs before live treatment.

# **6\. Technology & Software**

## **Core Software Stack**

**☐ Practice management / EMR / charting:** Scheduling, charting, intake, treatment notes, photography links, memberships/packages, reminders, deposits, and reporting should live in a system your team will actually use consistently.

**☐ POS and payment processing:** Support in-person payment, card on file, online invoices, financing if offered, package balances, refunds, tips if applicable, and integrated reporting.

**☐ Online booking:** Control what can be self-booked, how long each appointment blocks, lead times, consultation prerequisites, and prepayment/no-show rules.

**☐ Digital forms:** Intake, medical history, consent, privacy notices, treatment-specific protocols, aftercare, cancellation policy, and photography release should be digitized where possible.

**☐ Inventory tracking:** Even a simple inventory system beats guessing. Track retail stock, consumables, reorder points, and device-related consumables separately.

## **Communications and Infrastructure**

**☐ Phone system:** Dedicated business line, call routing, voicemail, text enablement if compliant with your workflow, headset support, and after-hours handling.

**☐ Website and domain:** Branded website, domain, email addresses, online booking link strategy, service pages, FAQ, pricing logic, forms, and SEO basics.

**☐ Wi-Fi and cybersecurity:** Separate staff and guest Wi-Fi, strong passwords, multi-factor authentication, firewall/router management, device inventory, and role-based access.

**☐ Printer/scanner and office tech:** All-in-one printer/scanner, label printer, document shredder, webcam/headset for tele-consults if used, and battery backup for network gear.

**☐ Cloud storage and backups:** Document storage, policy manuals, marketing files, vendor contracts, finance files, and photo backup policy with controlled permissions.

# **7\. Licensing, Compliance & Business Setup**

This section is a readiness map, not legal advice. Requirements can vary based on who performs services, physician supervision structure, service mix, property condition, municipality, and insurer expectations. Confirm directly with licensed counsel, accountant, architect/engineer, general contractor, landlord, insurer, and the relevant Florida/local authorities before relying on any item as complete.

## **Florida and Local Categories to Verify**

**☐ Business entity and tax setup:** Confirm business formation, EIN, state registration, local tax/accounting setup, sales tax treatment for taxable items if any, and banking/merchant accounts.

**☐ Zoning and allowed use:** Before investing in plans or equipment, confirm the intended use is permitted at the Lakeland address and that no special landlord or plaza restrictions apply.

**☐ Permits and Certificate of Occupancy:** Tenant improvements usually require permit review, inspections, and final approvals before lawful occupancy/opening. Confirm permit pathway early.

**☐ Professional licensing:** Verify the specific licensure and supervision requirements that apply to every service, every provider type, and every device or modality you intend to offer.

**☐ Laser/light-based hair removal requirements:** Florida requirements for laser/light-based hair removal, physician supervision, electrology licensure/facility licensure, protocols, and related documentation must be confirmed directly before opening.

## **Safety, Privacy, and Documentation**

**☐ OSHA setup:** Hazard communication, SDS access, bloodborne pathogen considerations if applicable, PPE, training records, exposure control documentation if applicable, and workplace incident procedures.

**☐ HIPAA/privacy:** Risk assessment, notice/privacy practices as applicable, minimum necessary access, secure device use, breach response planning, and staff confidentiality training.

**☐ Biomedical waste and sharps:** If your services generate sharps or biomedical waste, verify generator permit, storage, labeling, pickup, and training requirements.

**☐ Policies and manuals:** Prepare employee handbook, SOP manual, emergency procedures, cleaning protocols, incident log, maintenance logs, consent forms, treatment protocols, and complaint resolution process.

**☐ Insurance:** General liability, professional liability/malpractice as applicable, property, workers compensation, cyber/privacy, employment practices, business interruption, and equipment coverage.

# **Optional Enhancer A. Phase 1 vs Phase 2 Build-Out Plan**

A lean opening can protect cash without making the clinic feel unfinished. The trick is designing Phase 2 capacity during Phase 1, so future expansion is surgical instead of disruptive.

**☐ Phase 1 must-have:** Reception, waiting, consultation room, 2 treatment rooms, ADA restroom, compact workroom/laundry, core software, primary device, essential furniture, opening inventory, legal/compliance setup.

**☐ Phase 1 future-proofing:** Rough-in extra power/data, pre-plan future cabinetry dimensions, reserve one shell-ready room or flex room, choose finishes and signage scalable to future service additions.

**☐ Phase 2 can-phase-in:** Third/fourth treatment room activation, secondary device, upgraded retail display, premium millwork, décor upgrades, photo wall build-out, larger team, expanded service menu.

# **Missing-Items Risk List: Commonly Overlooked in Med Spa and Laser Clinic Build-Outs**

**☐ Device electrical specs obtained too late:** Can force expensive rework or extension cords nobody should be improvising around.

**☐ Insufficient storage:** Clinics rarely regret adding storage during design. They constantly regret skipping it.

**☐ Poor acoustic privacy:** Clients remember overheard conversations more vividly than your paint color.

**☐ No standardized photo workflow:** Inconsistent photos weaken marketing, treatment comparison, and expectation management.

**☐ Underestimating laundry and linen volume:** Aesthetic businesses create more textile chaos than people admit during planning.

**☐ Weak front-desk cable management:** A beautiful reception desk turns into a visible wire swamp fast.

**☐ No contingency budget:** Construction and opening phases treat optimism as prey.

**☐ No backup plan for device downtime:** Lost treatment days can snowball into cancellations, refunds, and review damage.

**☐ Incomplete SOPs and treatment protocols:** Staff make up their own system when yours is not written down.

**☐ Opening before software and forms are truly ready:** Chaos at check-in travels all the way through treatment and checkout.

**☐ Ignoring signage and landlord rules until late:** Storefront visibility and permit timing can delay launch or force redesign.

**☐ Too little working capital:** Opening day is not the finish line; it is the start of carrying payroll, rent, and marketing while demand ramps.
