# S.A.L.T. — Skin And Laser Treatment

## Comprehensive Brand Development Checklist & Action Plan

---

## 1. BRAND STRATEGY & FOUNDATION

### Mission Statement

Craft a one-to-two sentence declaration of what S.A.L.T. exists to do. For a med spa, this needs to bridge the gap between medical expertise and personal transformation.
A working example:

> *"S.A.L.T. exists to elevate skin health through precision laser and aesthetic treatments delivered in an environment where clinical excellence meets understated luxury."*

The mission should feel grounded and confident — never hyperbolic. It must resonate with clients who are research-driven, results-oriented, and aesthetically discerning.
The S.A.L.T. acronym lends itself to language around essential, elemental, foundational care — salt as something pure, necessary, and timeless. Lean into that.

### Vision Statement

Define where the brand is headed long-term. This is your north star for expansion decisions, new service additions, and culture building.
Consider something that positions S.A.L.T. as a leader in the intersection of dermatological science and modern self-care — a brand that normalizes clinical skin treatments as part of an elevated, routine wellness lifestyle rather than a vanity-driven indulgence.
The vision should be ambitious enough to allow for multi-location growth, product lines, or membership models down the road.

### Brand Values

Identify five to seven core values that govern every decision from hiring to treatment room playlist selection. For S.A.L.T., these should reflect the minimalist, modern positioning.
Suggested values to refine include:

- Precision (every treatment is intentional and evidence-based)
- Transparency (honest consultations, realistic expectations, clear pricing)
- Refinement (beauty in simplicity, never overdone)
- Warmth (clinical does not mean cold)
- Integrity (medical-grade standards without compromise)

Each value should have a short internal descriptor explaining what it looks like in practice so that every team member can internalize it.

### Brand Voice & Tone Guidelines

Document exactly how S.A.L.T. speaks. The voice should be consistent across all channels — confident, knowledgeable, calm, and subtly warm. It should never sound salesy, overly casual, or clinical to the point of being intimidating. Think of the brand as a trusted friend who happens to be a highly trained aesthetic professional. The tone shifts slightly by context: on social media it can be a touch more approachable and conversational; on the website it should feel editorial and polished; on intake forms it should be clear, reassuring, and professional. Write out "we say this, not that" examples. For instance, "treatment plan" rather than "package deal," "consultation" rather than "sales appointment," "skin health" rather than "anti-aging." Avoid fear-based language about aging or skin imperfections. The brand empowers; it does not shame.

### Ideal Client Personas

Develop two to three detailed personas representing the core clientele. For a med spa in this price and aesthetic tier, common archetypes include: the Professional Minimalist (30–45, high-earning, values efficiency and results, does her research, wants a provider she trusts so she can stop overthinking it), the Wellness-Invested Client (35–55, already sees a dermatologist and nutritionist, views skin treatments as part of her overall health protocol, values transparency and science-backed approaches), and the Newcomer (25–35, curious about med spa treatments but intimidated by the clinical aspect, needs education and a welcoming environment to take the first step). Each persona should include demographics, psychographics, media consumption habits, objections to overcome, and the emotional outcome they seek from treatment.

### Competitor Analysis Framework

Build a structured template for evaluating direct competitors (other med spas in your market) and indirect competitors (dermatology offices offering cosmetic services, high-end day spas, at-home device brands). Assess each on visual branding, price positioning, service menu breadth, online presence quality, Google review volume and sentiment, social media engagement, and stated value proposition. Identify whitespace — where competitors are falling short in either brand experience or clinical credibility — and position S.A.L.T. to fill that gap. Most med spas lean either too clinical (sterile, forgettable branding) or too glam (heavy filters, Kardashian-adjacent marketing). S.A.L.T. should own the sophisticated middle ground.

### Unique Value Proposition

Distill into one clear statement what makes S.A.L.T. different and why that difference matters to the client. This should combine the what (precision skin and laser treatments), the how (minimalist, modern, science-first environment), and the why it matters (clients get expert-level results in a space that feels intentional and elevated, not assembly-line aesthetic). The UVP should be testable — if you read it and it could apply to any other med spa, it is not specific enough.

### Brand Story & Narrative

Write the origin story of S.A.L.T. This is the narrative you will use on the About page, in press pitches, and during the launch campaign. It should answer why the founder(s) started this, what gap they saw in the market, and what conviction drives the brand. Weave in the meaning behind the name — the idea that salt is elemental, essential, purifying, and preserving. Salt is both ancient and endlessly relevant. The brand story should feel personal without being self-indulgent, and it should ultimately turn the spotlight back to the client and their experience.

### Positioning Statement

Create a formal positioning statement following the classic framework: For [target client] who [need/desire], S.A.L.T. is the [category] that [key benefit] because [reason to believe]. This is an internal strategic document, not client-facing copy, but it anchors all messaging. Example to refine: *"For discerning individuals who want results-driven skin treatments without the clinical coldness or oversaturated aesthetic of typical med spas, S.A.L.T. is the modern skin and laser studio that delivers expert care in a refined, intentional environment, because we believe effective treatment and elevated experience are not mutually exclusive."*

## 2. NAMING & VERBAL IDENTITY

### Acronym vs. Full Name Usage Rules

Establish clear hierarchy and usage rules. "S.A.L.T." is the primary brand name and should appear in the logo, signage, and all major touchpoints. "Skin And Laser Treatment" serves as a descriptor and should appear beneath or alongside the acronym on the website homepage, business cards, and any context where a first-time audience needs to understand what the business does. In running copy and conversation, "S.A.L.T." is sufficient. Decide whether the periods in the acronym are always used (more formal, more editorial) or dropped in certain casual contexts. Recommendation: always keep the periods in the logo and formal usage for visual distinction, but allow "SALT" without periods in hashtags and social handles where punctuation is impractical.

### Tagline Options

Develop three to five tagline candidates and test them. Taglines for a med spa should be short, memorable, and evocative without being cliché. Avoid anything that sounds like it belongs on a mass-market skincare bottle. Directions to explore: the essential/elemental angle (leaning into the salt metaphor), the precision/intentionality angle (reflecting the minimalist brand), or the results/transformation angle (keeping it about the client). Examples to workshop: *"Essential skin. Elevated care."* / *"Precision is the new luxury."* / *"Where science meets skin."* / *"The element of beautiful skin."* / *"Refined. Results-driven. Radical simplicity."* Test finalists with target persona representatives for resonance and clarity.

### Service & Treatment Naming Conventions

Create a naming system for your service menu that feels cohesive and on-brand. Many med spas default to clinical names (IPL Photofacial, Fractional CO2 Resurfacing) or overly whimsical names (The Glow-Up, The Time Machine). S.A.L.T. should find a middle path — names that are clean, descriptive, and slightly elevated. Consider a structure where each treatment has a branded name and a clinical descriptor. For example: "Resurface — Fractional Laser Skin Renewal" or "Clarify — IPL Photofacial Treatment." You could also extend the mineral/elemental theme into treatment tiers or collections (e.g., "The Essentials" for entry-level treatments, "The Reserves" for premium packages). Whatever system you choose, document it so that new services added in the future follow the same naming logic.

### Terminology Guide

Build a glossary of preferred language. This is critical for med spas because the way you talk about procedures, bodies, and outcomes shapes client trust and comfort. Document preferred terms and their alternatives. Use "treatment" rather than "procedure" in client-facing contexts (less clinical). Use "consultation" rather than "appointment" for first visits (implies expertise and conversation, not a transaction). Use "skin health" rather than "anti-aging" (positive framing). Use "enhancement" rather than "correction" (empowering rather than problem-focused). Use "provider" or "specialist" rather than "technician" (elevates the perceived expertise). This guide should be distributed to every team member and used in all copywriting.

### Tone-of-Voice Examples by Context

Provide written examples showing how the same message would be delivered across different channels. Take a scenario like promoting a new laser treatment: on the website, the copy might read, *"Our newest addition targets hyperpigmentation at its source, delivering visible clarity in as few as two sessions. Book a consultation to see if Clarify is right for your skin."* On Instagram, the same promotion might read, *"Dark spots? We see you. Our new Clarify treatment is here and the results are already speaking for themselves. Link in bio to learn more."* On a post-treatment follow-up email, it might read, *"Hi [Name], it was great seeing you yesterday. Your skin may feel slightly warm for the next 24 hours — completely normal. Here's your aftercare guide, and don't hesitate to reach out if anything comes up."* These examples train everyone from the front desk to the social media manager to stay in voice.

## 3. VISUAL IDENTITY SYSTEM

### Logo Design

Commission a logo suite that includes four to five variations: a primary logo (the full "S.A.L.T." wordmark with the descriptor "Skin And Laser Treatment"), a secondary logo (the wordmark without the descriptor for use where context is already established), a submark (a compact icon or monogram — possibly a stylized "S" or an abstract mark inspired by salt crystals or mineral geometry), and a favicon (a simplified version of the submark optimized for tiny digital display). The logo should be typographic-forward, reflecting the minimalist brand. Avoid overly decorative elements, swooshes, or generic medical/spa iconography like leaves or water droplets. The letterforms themselves should carry the brand's personality — clean, contemporary, and slightly architectural. Consider custom-modified typography for the wordmark to ensure uniqueness. Deliver all logo files in vector (AI, EPS, SVG), high-resolution PNG with transparent background, and single-color versions in both dark and light for flexibility. A med spa logo needs to work at large scale on building signage and at small scale embossed on product packaging. Test every variation at both extremes. Tools and vendors: hire a brand identity designer or studio with experience in hospitality, beauty, or wellness branding. Platforms like Dribbble and Behance are good for sourcing designers. Budget range for a full identity system from a mid-tier studio is typically \$3,000–\$15,000 depending on scope.

### Color Palette

Define a full palette with specific hex, RGB, and CMYK values for each color. The palette should include a primary neutral group (three to four tones), one to two moody accent colors, and functional tones for digital UI elements. Suggested starting point for refinement: a warm white like Alabaster (#F2F0EB) as the primary background, a soft taupe or greige (#C4B8A8) for secondary backgrounds and dividers, a deep charcoal or off-black (#2C2C2C) for primary text, a warm stone (#A39382) for secondary text and subtle accents, and then one to two vivid deep accents — a smoked burgundy (#6B2D3E) or a deep teal (#2A5C5A) for calls to action, highlights, and moments of visual drama. Establish usage ratios: approximately 60% neutrals, 25% stone/taupe midtones, 15% accent. The accent color should be used sparingly so that when it appears it carries impact — a booking button, a treatment room accent wall, a gift card envelope liner. For a med spa, the palette must read as clean and trustworthy while also feeling luxurious. Avoid palettes that skew too cold (feels clinical) or too pastel (feels soft and unserious).

### Typography System

Select two to three typefaces that work in harmony. A recommended structure: a serif or refined sans-serif for headings (something with personality and editorial elegance, such as a font in the vein of Cormorant Garamond, Freight Display, or Didot for serif direction, or Monument Extended, Neue Haas Grotesk, or PP Mori for sans-serif direction), a clean sans-serif for body copy (Inter, Söhne, Helvetica Neue, or Aktiv Grotesk all work well for readability at small sizes), and optionally a refined accent font for special uses like pull quotes, monograms, or packaging (a light-weight didone or a subtle display face). Specify exact weights to use — for example, Light for large display headings, Regular for body, Medium for subheadings, and Bold only for critical emphasis. Define line-height, letter-spacing, and capitalization rules. The acronym "S.A.L.T." will likely look strongest in all-caps with tracked-out letter spacing. Ensure all typefaces are licensed appropriately for both web and print use. Google Fonts offers strong free options; Adobe Fonts and foundry licensing provide access to premium typefaces.

### Iconography Style

Define a custom icon style for use across the website, service menus, social media, and signage. For S.A.L.T., icons should be line-based, minimal, and consistent in stroke weight. They should feel architectural rather than illustrative — geometric rather than organic. Use icons to represent service categories (a simplified laser beam for laser treatments, a droplet for injectables, a face outline for facials, etc.), website navigation, and infographic-style content. If custom icons are beyond budget at launch, select a high-quality icon set that matches the style and customize as needed. Noun Project and Streamline Icons are good resources.

### Pattern & Texture Library

Develop a small library of brand patterns and textures for use as backgrounds, packaging accents, and digital design elements. Drawing from the salt/mineral concept, consider subtle crystal-like geometric patterns, fine-grain textures reminiscent of salt flats or mineral surfaces, or abstract topographic line patterns that evoke a natural-yet-modern feeling. These should be used sparingly and never compete with the minimal aesthetic — think of them as quiet details that reward closer inspection. They work beautifully on the interior of envelopes, behind website section headers, or as faint watermarks on printed collateral.

### Photography Direction & Mood Board

Create a comprehensive photography direction document. For S.A.L.T., imagery should follow these principles: lighting should be soft, natural, and directional (think morning light from a large window — warm but not orange, bright but not flat), compositions should be clean with generous negative space, color grading should feel true to life but slightly desaturated with warm undertones (avoid heavy filtering or the orange-and-teal Instagram look), skin should look like real skin (not airbrushed to plastic perfection — show texture, show pores, show realness, since this builds trust for a med spa), and environments should be styled minimally (no clutter, no props for the sake of props). Subjects should look calm and self-possessed, never performatively happy or dramatically "before." Build two mood boards: one for clinical/treatment imagery (the space, the equipment, the providers at work — conveying expertise and precision) and one for lifestyle/brand imagery (textures, abstract details, hands, skin close-ups, architectural details — conveying mood and aspiration). Stock photography should be avoided where possible. Invest in a professional brand shoot before launch, using a photographer experienced in beauty, interiors, or editorial work. Budget range: \$1,500–\$5,000 for a full-day shoot with a small creative team.

### Brand Style Guide Document

Compile all of the above into a single, comprehensive brand style guide (also called brand book or brand standards). This document should be designed and formatted beautifully — it is a reflection of the brand itself. Include sections for logo usage (including minimum clear space, minimum sizes, and incorrect usage examples), color palette with all specifications, typography with hierarchy examples, photography and imagery guidelines with sample images, icon usage, pattern library, voice and tone overview, and collateral templates. The style guide is a living document that should be updated as the brand evolves, and it should be distributed to every designer, copywriter, printer, and marketing partner who touches the brand. Deliver it as both a PDF and an editable format (InDesign or Figma file). For a med spa with plans to grow, this document is essential for maintaining consistency as you add locations, hire new staff, or bring on external marketing help.

## 4. COLLATERAL & PRINT DESIGN

### Business Cards

Design business cards that feel like a brand experience in miniature. For S.A.L.T., consider a heavier card stock (16pt or thicker), a matte or soft-touch finish (aligns with the minimalist luxury aesthetic far better than glossy), and a possible accent detail like an edge paint in the brand's accent color, a blind deboss of the submark, or a foil stamp on the logo. The front should carry the S.A.L.T. logo and the team member's name and title. The back should include contact information, website URL, and booking link or QR code. Keep the layout clean with generous white space. For a med spa, the business card is often handed to a client at the end of a consultation — it is the last physical impression and should reinforce the quality and intentionality of the experience. Print vendor recommendation: look for a specialty printer like MOO, Mama's Sauce, or a local letterpress studio for premium finishing options.

### Letterhead & Envelopes

Design branded letterhead for formal correspondence, referral letters to dermatologists or physicians, and internal documents. The letterhead should feature the primary logo at the top, contact information at the bottom, and minimal design — let the neutrals and typography do the work. Matching envelopes should include the logo on the flap or front and, if budget allows, a lined interior featuring one of the brand textures or a wash of the accent color. These details matter for a med spa because you may be communicating with referring physicians, insurance entities, or high-value clients, and every touchpoint should reinforce professionalism and brand quality.

### Appointment Cards

Design small-format appointment reminder cards to hand to clients at checkout. Include space for the next appointment date and time, the provider's name, and a brief line about any pre-treatment instructions. Include the S.A.L.T. logo and booking phone number or URL. Even in an era of digital scheduling, a physical card serves as a tactile reminder and brand reinforcement. Use the same premium card stock and finish as the business cards for cohesion.

### Gift Cards & Certificates

Design a branded gift card system. This is a significant revenue driver for med spas, especially during holidays. The physical gift card should feel luxurious — consider packaging it in a branded envelope or sleeve with the accent-color interior lining. The card itself can be a thick stock card with foil or deboss details, or a durable PVC card if integrating with a POS system. Design a matching digital gift card for online purchases, maintaining the same visual language. Include clear redemption instructions and terms. The gift card presentation should feel like giving someone a high-end gift, not a generic Visa card.

### Intake & Consent Forms

Redesign standard medical intake and consent forms to reflect the S.A.L.T. brand. Most med spas use generic, text-heavy forms that feel clinical and anxiety-inducing. S.A.L.T.'s forms should be cleanly typeset with the brand's body typeface, include the logo as a header, use clear section headings, and provide adequate white space so they do not feel overwhelming. If using digital intake forms (recommended for efficiency and data management), ensure the digital platform can be branded with your colors, logo, and fonts. Platforms like IntakeQ, Aesthetic Record, or PatientNow allow varying degrees of customization. This matters because the intake process is often a client's first hands-on interaction with the brand after booking, and a confusing or ugly form undermines the elevated experience you are building.

### Brochures & Service Menu

Design a printed service guide that clients can take home from a consultation or that can be mailed as part of a welcome package. This should not be a typical tri-fold brochure — consider a small booklet format (A5 or similar) or an oversized single-fold card that feels editorial. Organize treatments by category with brief, benefit-focused descriptions (not clinical jargon), price ranges (or note that pricing is provided during consultation, depending on your pricing transparency philosophy), and a clear call to action to book. The design should feature the brand photography, generous spacing, and the moody accent color used sparingly for emphasis. This piece does significant selling work for clients who need to consider their options before committing, so it must look and feel premium enough that they keep it rather than toss it.

### Packaging

If S.A.L.T. retails skincare products (its own line or curated partner brands), design branded packaging elements. These could include branded bags (a matte-finish paper bag with a simple logo print), tissue paper (white or stone-colored with a subtle repeating submark pattern), stickers or seals (for closing tissue paper or sealing boxes), and product labels if launching a house line. Even if you only retail other brands' products, wrapping them in branded packaging transforms a purchase into a branded experience. This is especially important for med spas where retail can account for 20–30% of revenue — the packaging sells the next purchase before the product even does.

### Signage Templates

Develop a signage system including exterior building signage (primary logo, likely illuminated or dimensional lettering), a window decal or vinyl with hours and contact information, interior wayfinding signs (reception, treatment rooms, restrooms, retail area), and digital or printed signs for treatment room doors, retail product information cards, and promotional easel displays. All signage should adhere to the brand style guide — same fonts, colors, and minimal aesthetic. For exterior signage, work with a licensed sign company and verify local permitting and landlord requirements early in the process, as production and installation can take four to eight weeks.

### Branded Merchandise

Consider a small run of branded merchandise for client gifting, staff use, or retail. For a minimalist med spa brand, appropriate merchandise includes a branded candle (extending sensory branding into a take-home product), a high-quality reusable water bottle or tumbler (salt and hydration are a natural pairing), a branded tote bag (functional and visible), or a simple branded robe or headband for use during treatments and available for purchase. Keep the branding subtle — a tone-on-tone logo or a small submark rather than a large splashy print. The merchandise should feel like something the client would genuinely want to use, not like a promotional giveaway.

## 5. DIGITAL PRESENCE

### Website Design & Development

The website is the single most important brand asset for a med spa. The majority of prospective clients will visit the site before booking, and many will form their entire first impression there. The site should be designed and built with the following structure and considerations.

**Key pages include:** a homepage that immediately communicates what S.A.L.T. is, who it is for, and what makes it different, featuring hero imagery, the tagline, a brief positioning statement, featured treatments, and a prominent booking CTA; a comprehensive treatments/services section with individual pages for each treatment category containing benefit-focused descriptions, expected results, recovery information, FAQ, and before-and-after galleries; an About page with the brand story, the team's credentials and photos, and the brand values; a gallery or results page showcasing real client outcomes; a blog or education hub for SEO and client education; a contact page with embedded map, hours, phone, email, and booking link; a booking/consultation page integrated with your scheduling platform; and a membership or loyalty program page if applicable.

**UX considerations:** the site must load fast (under three seconds), be fully responsive for mobile (over 60% of med spa traffic is mobile), feature intuitive navigation with no more than six to seven primary menu items, include sticky or persistent booking CTAs on every page, and use a clean layout with generous white space consistent with the minimalist brand. The booking integration is critical — use a platform like Vagaro, Boulevard, Mangomint, or Aesthetic Record that allows embedded or branded booking flows so the client never feels like they have left the S.A.L.T. experience. Avoid cheap-looking third-party booking pop-ups.

**Platform recommendation:** a custom WordPress build with Elementor or a Webflow build offers the best balance of design flexibility, performance, and long-term manageability for a single-location med spa. Squarespace is a viable lower-budget alternative if the template is heavily customized. Avoid Wix for a brand at this positioning level. Budget range: \$5,000–\$20,000 depending on complexity and whether you hire a freelancer or an agency.

### SEO Strategy

Develop a search engine optimization plan targeting local search intent. For a med spa, the highest-value keywords combine treatment names with geographic modifiers (e.g., "laser skin resurfacing [city name]," "med spa near me," "Botox [neighborhood]"). Conduct keyword research using tools like SEMrush, Ahrefs, or Ubersuggest to identify the highest-volume, achievable keywords in your market. Optimize every treatment page with unique meta titles and descriptions, properly structured heading tags, image alt text, and internal linking. Build out long-form blog content targeting informational queries that prospective clients search before booking (e.g., "how long does laser hair removal take," "is microneedling worth it," "best treatments for melasma"). Ensure the site has proper schema markup for local business, medical business, and FAQ. SEO is a long game — plan for consistent content production for at least the first twelve months and monitor rankings monthly.

### Blog & Content Hub

Plan a content hub on the website that serves both SEO and client education. Content categories should include treatment deep-dives (what to expect, how it works, ideal candidates), skin science education (how the skin barrier works, the science of collagen, understanding pigmentation), myth-busting articles (addressing common misconceptions about laser treatments, injectables, and skincare), pre- and post-treatment care guides (doubling as practical resources for existing clients), and seasonal content (skin prep for summer, winter hydration, holiday gift guides). Aim to publish two to four articles per month at launch and at least two per month ongoing. Each article should be optimized for a target keyword, include relevant internal links to treatment pages, and conclude with a CTA to book a consultation.

### Email Marketing System

Set up an email marketing platform (Mailchimp, Klaviyo, or Flodesk are all appropriate depending on sophistication needs) and design branded email templates that match the website aesthetic. Plan the following automated sequences: a welcome series (three to four emails for new subscribers or first-time clients introducing the brand, highlighting popular treatments, and offering a new-client incentive), appointment reminders (automated through your scheduling platform but branded), post-treatment follow-ups (sent 24–48 hours after a treatment with aftercare instructions and a personal touch), re-engagement emails (targeting clients who haven't booked in 60–90 days), and promotional campaigns (monthly or seasonal promotions, new treatment announcements, events). Email is one of the highest-ROI marketing channels for med spas because the client lifetime value is high and repeat booking is the primary revenue driver. Every email should reflect the brand voice — warm, expert, never pushy.

### Social Media Profile Setup

Reserve and set up branded profiles on Instagram (primary platform for med spas), TikTok (growing rapidly for aesthetic education content), Facebook (necessary for ads and an older demographic), Pinterest (strong for aspirational beauty and wellness content), and LinkedIn (optional, but useful for recruiting and B2B credibility). Use a consistent handle across all platforms — @saltskinandlaser or @salt.medspa or similar, depending on availability. Design profile photos using the submark logo on a neutral background. Write bios that are concise, include the full name, location, and a booking link. Use a link-in-bio tool like Linktree or Stan Store (or a custom-built landing page for stronger branding) to direct traffic from social profiles to key pages.

### Online Directory Listings

Claim and optimize listings on all relevant directories including Google Business Profile, Yelp, RealSelf, Healthgrades, Zocdoc (if applicable), and any local or regional directories. Ensure NAP (Name, Address, Phone) consistency across all listings — inconsistent information damages local SEO. Upload branded photos, write keyword-rich descriptions, select accurate categories, and set up appointment links. Google Business Profile is the single most impactful directory for a med spa. Optimize it aggressively: upload twenty or more high-quality photos, post weekly Google updates, collect reviews actively (see the Client Experience section), respond to every review, and use the Q&A feature to preemptively answer common questions.

## 6. SOCIAL MEDIA & CONTENT

### Platform Strategy

Instagram is the primary platform for a med spa brand and should receive the majority of content effort. It is the platform where prospective clients research providers, view results, and assess brand aesthetic. TikTok is the growth platform for educational and behind-the-scenes content, with strong potential for viral reach. Facebook functions primarily as an advertising platform and a place for an older demographic to find and review your business. Pinterest is a long-game SEO play — pins have a much longer lifespan than social posts, and beauty and wellness content performs well on the platform. Allocate your effort accordingly: approximately 50% to Instagram, 25% to TikTok, 15% to Facebook, and 10% to Pinterest.

### Content Pillars

Define five to six content themes that all social content will fall under. This creates variety while maintaining strategic focus. Recommended pillars for S.A.L.T.: Education (explaining how treatments work, debunking myths, skin science), Results (before-and-after showcases, client testimonials, progress stories), Behind the Scenes (treatment process footage, the space, the team at work, day-in-the-life content), Brand World (aesthetic flat lays, texture close-ups, mood-driven content that communicates the S.A.L.T. visual identity without selling anything directly), Community (client spotlights, team introductions, local partnerships, event recaps), and Offers (promotions, new treatment announcements, membership highlights — kept to no more than 15–20% of total content so the feed does not feel transactional).

### Posting Cadence

For launch phase (first 90 days): Instagram feed posts three to four times per week, Instagram Stories daily, Instagram Reels three to four per week, TikTok three to five times per week, Facebook two to three times per week (can cross-post from Instagram), and Pinterest five to ten pins per week. Post-launch steady state: Instagram feed two to three times per week, Stories daily, Reels two to three per week, TikTok two to three per week, and Pinterest five to ten per week. Consistency matters more than volume. Use a scheduling tool like Later, Planoly, or Metricool to batch-create and schedule content.

### Reels & Short-Form Video Concepts

Short-form video is the highest-performing content type across both Instagram and TikTok. Develop a library of repeatable video concepts: a "What is [Treatment]?" series where a provider explains a treatment in sixty seconds or less over footage of the treatment being performed, a "Watch Me Work" series showing satisfying treatment footage (laser passes, extraction, skin transformation) with minimal text overlay and calming music, a "Your Questions Answered" series addressing FAQs pulled from DMs and consultations, a "Skin Tip of the Week" with a provider on camera sharing one actionable piece of advice, and a "Day at S.A.L.T." lifestyle vlog showing the space, a treatment in progress, and the client experience from arrival to departure. Keep the visual style consistent with the brand — muted tones, clean compositions, minimal graphic overlays. Avoid trendy audio or meme formats that conflict with the brand's sophistication unless they can be executed tastefully.

### Before-and-After Showcase Framework

Before-and-after content is the most persuasive content a med spa can produce, but it must be handled carefully for both credibility and compliance. Establish a standardized photography protocol: same lighting, same angle, same distance, same camera, no makeup in the "before" or "after," and consistent framing every time. Use a dedicated space or setup for B&A photos so the background is always clean and branded. Always obtain written consent from the client for use of their images. When posting, include the treatment name, number of sessions, and any relevant disclaimer language required by your state medical board or the FTC. Present B&A content respectfully — the "before" is not a problem to be ashamed of, and the "after" is not a miracle. Factual, understated presentation builds more trust than dramatic reveals.

### Educational Content Themes

Build a content calendar of educational topics organized by treatment area: laser treatments (how different laser wavelengths work, what downtime looks like, who is a candidate), injectables if offered (how neurotoxins differ from fillers, what a unit actually is, longevity of results), skin health fundamentals (the role of SPF, understanding ingredient labels, the skin barrier explained), and treatment planning (how to build a long-term skin health plan, combining treatments for best results, when to start various treatments). Educational content positions S.A.L.T. as a trusted authority and generates the type of high-save, high-share engagement that the algorithm rewards.

### Influencer & Partnership Strategy

Identify local micro-influencers (5,000–50,000 followers) in adjacent lifestyle categories — fitness, wellness, fashion, motherhood, real estate, local food and culture — who align with the S.A.L.T. aesthetic and client persona. Offer complimentary treatments in exchange for authentic content (stories and a feed post or Reel) with clear deliverables outlined in a simple agreement. Prioritize influencers whose audience demographics match your target client. Avoid influencers with heavily filtered or unrealistic aesthetic standards, as this undermines the brand's authenticity positioning. Also explore partnerships with complementary local businesses — a boutique fitness studio, a clean beauty retailer, a women-focused coworking space — for cross-promotion and co-hosted events.

### Hashtag Strategy

Develop a hashtag system organized into tiers: branded hashtags (#SALTmedspa, #SALTskin, #SkinAndLaserTreatment), industry hashtags (#medspa, #laserskincare, #aesthetictreatments, #skinhealth), niche hashtags (#minimalbeauty, #skinbarrier, #cleanskincare, #evidencebasedskincare), and local hashtags (#[city]medspa, #[city]beauty, #[city]skincare, #[neighborhood]). Use ten to fifteen hashtags per Instagram post, mixing tiers. Refresh the hashtag pool monthly based on performance data.

### UGC Guidelines

Create a framework for encouraging, collecting, and reposting user-generated content. Make it easy for clients to share their experience: a branded hashtag, a photogenic space (at least one "selfie moment" spot in the clinic), and a post-treatment prompt from front desk staff ("We'd love it if you shared your experience — tag us @saltskinandlaser!"). When reposting UGC, always credit the original creator, ensure the content aligns with the brand aesthetic (it's okay to be selective), and pair it with a caption that adds context or education. UGC is powerful social proof and reduces the content creation burden on your team.

## 7. PHYSICAL SPACE & ENVIRONMENT

### Interior Design Direction

The physical space must be the three-dimensional expression of the brand. Work with an interior designer (ideally one experienced in hospitality, wellness, or retail environments) to create a space that reflects the minimalist, modern, moody-accent identity. The reception area should feel like entering a high-end boutique hotel — not a doctor's office. Key design elements to consider: a neutral material palette (plaster or limewash walls in warm white or soft stone, natural oak or walnut millwork, concrete or stone flooring, fluted glass or reeded panels for texture), moody accent moments (a deep-toned accent wall in the consultation room, the accent color on the interior of cabinetry that is revealed when opened, a dramatic pendant light fixture), minimalist furniture (clean-line seating in neutral textiles, a sculptural reception desk), curated decor (one or two pieces of abstract art rather than gallery walls, a single statement vase rather than scattered accessories), and abundant but controlled lighting (warm-toned ambient lighting with task lighting in treatment rooms that can shift from warm to clinical as needed). Treatment rooms should feel private and cocoon-like — slightly darker, slightly warmer, with excellent sound insulation. The retail area should be integrated into the reception or waiting area with product displayed on minimal shelving, well-lit and easy to browse.

### Sensory Branding

Develop a multisensory brand experience that extends beyond visuals. Scent: commission or select a signature scent for the space — something subtle, clean, and grounding rather than sweet or floral. Notes like sea salt, sandalwood, white tea, cedar, or bergamot align with the brand identity. Use a cold-air diffusion system for consistent, not-overpowering scent distribution. Consider extending the signature scent into a branded candle for retail. Sound: create a branded playlist or work with a music curation service (Soundtrack Your Brand, Rockbot, or a custom Spotify playlist) to develop a consistent sonic identity — ambient, downtempo, instrumental, with organic textures. No top-40, no identifiable lyrics, no abrupt mood shifts. The music should feel like a natural extension of the space. Touch: consider the tactile details — the weight and texture of the robe, the softness of the treatment bed linens, the feel of the consultation room chair. These details register subconsciously and contribute to perceived quality.

### Staff Uniform & Dress Code

Design or specify a uniform system that reinforces the brand. For treatment providers, consider a modern, fitted scrub in the brand's charcoal or stone tone — brands like FIGS, Jaanuu, or Mandala Scrubs offer elevated options, or commission custom scrubs in the exact brand color. For front desk and non-clinical staff, specify a dress code aligned with the aesthetic: neutral tones (black, white, taupe, stone), minimal jewelry, clean lines. Provide branded elements like a name badge with the S.A.L.T. submark and optionally a branded lightweight jacket or vest. The overall effect should be polished, cohesive, and approachable — a team that looks like they belong in this space.

### In-Office Signage & Wayfinding

Design interior signage that is functional and beautiful. Room identifiers should use the brand typeface on a clean background — consider dimensional lettering or engraved plaques for a premium feel. Wayfinding should be intuitive enough that a first-time client can navigate comfortably without asking. Restroom signage, Wi-Fi information, and any policy notices should all be branded and well-designed rather than handwritten or printed on plain paper. A single piece of poorly designed signage can undermine an otherwise excellent physical brand.

### Retail Display Design

Design a retail display system that showcases products as curated, desirable objects rather than pharmacy-shelf inventory. Use consistent shelving, good lighting (accent spotlights or backlit shelving), and clean product information cards designed in the brand system. Group products by concern or routine rather than brand if carrying multiple lines. Include a tester station with branded mirrors and disposable applicators. Staff should be trained to make personalized product recommendations as an extension of the treatment experience, not as a hard sell.

### Client Experience Touchpoints

Map every physical touchpoint from the moment a client approaches the building to the moment they leave, and ensure each one is branded and intentional. This includes: the exterior approach and signage (is the entrance clearly marked and welcoming?), the door and entry (is the first thing they see the reception desk or a chaotic hallway?), the greeting (are they welcomed by name if possible?), the waiting experience (what do they see, hear, smell, sit on, read?), the consultation room (is it private, comfortable, and equipped with visual aids for treatment education?), the treatment room (is the bed made perfectly, the lighting set, the music playing?), the treatment itself (is the provider communicating clearly, checking comfort?), the post-treatment moment (are they offered water, a mirror, aftercare instructions?), the checkout experience (is it smooth, unhurried, with the next appointment offered proactively?), and the departure (are they thanked warmly and walked to the door?). Document these touchpoints and train every staff member on the expected experience at each stage.

## 8. CLIENT EXPERIENCE & OPERATIONS BRANDING

### Client Journey Mapping

Create a detailed client journey map that tracks every interaction from first awareness to loyal repeat client. The stages are: Awareness (how they discover S.A.L.T. — social media, Google, referral, driving past), Consideration (visiting the website, reading reviews, comparing to competitors), Booking (the ease and speed of scheduling, confirmation communication), Pre-Appointment (what happens between booking and showing up — reminder emails, intake forms, preparation instructions), Arrival and Treatment (the in-person experience mapped above), Post-Treatment (follow-up communication, aftercare support), Retention (rebooking prompts, loyalty program, ongoing education), and Advocacy (referral program, review requests, social sharing). At each stage, identify the client's emotional state, their key questions or concerns, the brand touchpoints involved, and opportunities to exceed expectations. This map becomes the blueprint for everything from email sequences to staff training.

### Consultation Process Branding

The initial consultation is the highest-leverage moment in the client journey for a med spa. It is where trust is built (or lost) and where treatment plans are sold through education rather than pressure. Brand this experience deliberately. The consultation room should be comfortable and private, not clinical and fluorescent. The provider should have a branded treatment planning template — a physical or digital document that outlines the client's concerns, goals, recommended treatments, expected timeline, and investment. Present this as a personalized plan, not a price list. Consider a branded "skin analysis" process using technology (like a Visia skin analysis system) presented on a screen with the S.A.L.T. visual identity framing the results. The consultation should feel like visiting a knowledgeable, caring expert — not like sitting through a sales pitch.

### Pre- and Post-Appointment Communication Templates

Write branded templates for every automated and manual communication. Pre-appointment: a booking confirmation email (warm, clear, with date/time/location, what to expect, and how to prepare), a reminder email or SMS 48 hours before, and a day-of reminder. Post-appointment: a same-day or next-day follow-up email from the provider with personalized aftercare instructions, a check-in email at the three-to-five day mark (especially for treatments with downtime), and a rebooking prompt at the appropriate interval based on the treatment received. Each template should be written in the S.A.L.T. voice, designed with the brand's visual identity, and feel personal even when automated.

### Loyalty & Membership Program

Design a loyalty or membership structure that encourages repeat visits and increases client lifetime value. Two common models for med spas: a points-based loyalty program (clients earn points per dollar spent, redeemable for treatments or products) or a monthly membership program (clients pay a recurring monthly fee in exchange for a set treatment or credit amount plus member-exclusive perks like discounted add-ons, priority booking, and members-only events). Name the program in line with the brand — "The Vault" (playing on salt vaults/reserves), "S.A.L.T. Membership," "The Reserve," or "Essential Membership." Design a branded membership card (physical or digital) and a dedicated section on the website explaining the program, benefits, and tiers if applicable. The financial structure should be modeled carefully to ensure profitability — consult with a med spa business consultant or accountant to set pricing and terms.

### Review & Testimonial Collection Strategy

Develop a systematic approach to collecting reviews. Online reviews are among the most influential factors in a prospective client's decision for a med spa. Implement a process where every client is prompted to leave a review following their appointment — this can be done via an automated email or SMS sent a few hours post-treatment, containing a direct link to your Google Business Profile review page. Train front desk staff to mention it during checkout in a natural, non-pressured way. For longer-form testimonials, periodically invite highly satisfied clients to share their story via a brief written Q&A or a short video testimonial. Offer a small incentive (a product sample, a discount on their next visit) in exchange for video testimonials. Feature reviews prominently on the website, in social media content, and in the physical space (a framed quote or digital display in the waiting area).

### Referral Program

Design a referral program that rewards existing clients for bringing new ones. The program should be simple, generous, and easy to participate in. A common effective structure: the referring client receives a credit toward their next treatment (e.g., \$50), and the referred new client receives a first-visit incentive (e.g., \$50 off their first treatment). Provide branded referral cards (physical, sized like a business card) that existing clients can hand to friends, as well as a digital referral link or code they can share via text or social media. Track referrals in your CRM or booking system. Promote the program on the website, in post-treatment emails, and with small signage at the front desk.

### Branded Onboarding Experience

Create a memorable first-visit experience that goes beyond the clinical. Consider a branded welcome kit for first-time clients: a small branded bag or box containing a sample-sized product (sunscreen is universally relevant and reinforces skin health), an aftercare guide, a service menu booklet, a referral card, and a handwritten welcome note from the team. This does not need to be expensive — the value is in the presentation and the intentionality. First-time clients who feel special and seen are far more likely to return and refer.

## 9. MARKETING & LAUNCH PLAN

### Pre-Launch Teaser Campaign (8–6 Weeks Before Opening)

Build anticipation before the doors open. Set up the Instagram and TikTok accounts and begin posting brand-world content: construction progress, material selections, mood shots, the story behind the name, and team introductions. Do not reveal everything at once — drip information to build curiosity. Use a "Coming Soon" landing page on the website with an email signup to capture early interest and offer first-access booking to the waitlist. Tease the space with close-up details rather than full reveals. Use the branded hashtag from day one. If budget allows, hire a photographer or videographer to document the build-out process for a launch video. Leverage the founder's personal social media network to amplify awareness.

### Grand Opening Event (Opening Week)

Host an in-person launch event that introduces the brand, the space, and the team to the local community. Invite local influencers, potential referral partners (dermatologists, OB-GYNs, plastic surgeons, fitness studio owners), press, and the email waitlist. The event should embody the brand: elegant but not stuffy, with minimal decor, on-brand food and drink (think clean, sophisticated — a salt-rimmed cocktail is an obvious brand-aligned choice), guided tours of the treatment rooms, live mini-demonstrations (a skin analysis station, a laser demo on a team member), and an event-exclusive booking incentive. Hire a photographer and content creator to document the event for social media and PR use. Send a press release to local media, lifestyle blogs, and any beauty or wellness editors in your market.

### Launch-Day Promotions

Offer a compelling but not discount-dependent opening promotion. Avoid slashing prices dramatically, as this sets a precedent that undermines perceived value. Instead, offer added value: a complimentary skin analysis with every consultation booked in the first month, a free treatment add-on with any first booking, or a founding member rate for the membership program that rewards early adopters with a locked-in lower monthly rate. Create urgency with a limited-time window.

### PR & Media Outreach

Build a targeted press list including local lifestyle magazine editors, newspaper lifestyle section writers, local TV morning show producers, regional beauty and wellness bloggers, and podcast hosts in your market. Prepare a press kit containing: a one-page brand fact sheet (who, what, where, why, what makes it different), the brand story, high-resolution brand photographs, founder biography and headshot, and a clear media contact. Pitch a variety of story angles — the opening of a new business, the design story of the space, an expert piece authored by the founder on a skin health topic, or a trend piece on the rise of science-first aesthetics. Follow up personally and offer complimentary treatments for editors to experience firsthand.

### Local Partnership Opportunities

Identify ten to fifteen local businesses and organizations for cross-promotion partnerships. Ideal partners include boutique fitness studios (CrossFit, Pilates, yoga, cycling), women-focused coworking spaces, high-end hair salons, luxury real estate agents (for client gifting), bridal boutiques (for wedding-prep treatment packages), wellness practitioners (functional medicine, acupuncture, nutrition), and upscale hotels or spas that do not offer medical aesthetics. Partnership structures can include co-hosted events, cross-promotional social media features, shared referral incentives, and branded collateral placement in each other's spaces.

### Paid Advertising Strategy

Launch paid campaigns on Meta (Instagram and Facebook) and Google simultaneously. For Meta, start with awareness-focused campaigns targeting women 25–55 in your geographic radius who have interests in skincare, beauty, wellness, dermatology, and competing med spa brands. Use high-quality brand photography and short video content. Retarget website visitors and engaged social media audiences with booking-focused conversion campaigns. For Google, run search ads targeting high-intent keywords like "med spa near me," "laser treatment [city]," "[specific treatment] [city]," and branded terms. Also set up a Performance Max campaign tied to the Google Business Profile. Start with a combined monthly budget of \$2,000–\$5,000, allocating approximately 60% to Meta and 40% to Google, and optimize based on cost-per-lead data after the first 30 days. Consider hiring a media buyer experienced in med spa advertising to manage these campaigns — the regulatory nuances of aesthetic advertising (especially around before-and-after imagery and medical claims) require specialized knowledge.

### Direct Mail Campaign

Design and send a branded direct mail piece to high-value zip codes in your target area. A well-designed oversized postcard or a fold-out mailer with a first-visit offer and a QR code linking to the booking page can be effective for local awareness, particularly for an older demographic or affluent neighborhoods where physical mail still carries weight. Use a service like PostcardMania, Taradel, or a local direct mail company. Target 5,000–10,000 households within a defined radius. Include a trackable offer code to measure response rate.

### Community Engagement Tactics

Plan grassroots marketing activities that build local brand awareness and goodwill. Sponsor or participate in local wellness events, charity runs, women's networking groups, or school fundraisers. Offer to host educational workshops ("The Science of SPF" or "Understanding Your Skin Type") at partner businesses or in your own space. Create a quarterly "community highlight" feature on social media spotlighting a local business you admire. These activities build genuine relationships and word-of-mouth referrals that paid advertising cannot replicate.

### 90-Day Post-Launch Marketing Calendar

Build a week-by-week calendar for the first 90 days covering all marketing channels. Weeks one through two should focus on grand opening momentum — event content, press coverage amplification, launch promotion push, and heavy social posting. Weeks three through four should shift to social proof — posting early client testimonials, before-and-after content (with appropriate consent and timelines for results to appear), and referral program promotion. Weeks five through eight should build authority — ramp up educational blog content, begin the email newsletter cadence, and publish the first long-form video content. Weeks nine through twelve should focus on optimization and retention — analyze advertising performance and reallocate budget, launch or promote the membership program, plan the first post-launch client event or seasonal promotion, and review all KPIs (website traffic, booking conversion rate, social media growth, email list growth, revenue per client, client return rate). This calendar should be a living document reviewed and updated weekly.

## 10. LEGAL & ADMINISTRATIVE BRANDING

### Trademark Search & Registration

Before investing heavily in brand assets, conduct a thorough trademark search to ensure "S.A.L.T." and "Skin And Laser Treatment" are available for use in your class (likely Class 44 — medical and beauty services). Search the USPTO database (TESS), state trademark databases, and do a broad common-law search including Google, social media, and domain registrars. Because "salt" is a common English word, there may be existing registrations in adjacent categories, but med spa services are specific enough that you may have a clear path. File a federal trademark application as soon as feasibility is confirmed. Work with a trademark attorney — this is not a do-it-yourself task. Cost is typically \$500–\$2,000 for the attorney plus \$250–\$350 per class in USPTO filing fees. The registration process takes eight to twelve months, but you gain rights from the filing date.

### Domain Name Registration

Secure the primary website domain and any protective variants. Ideal primary domains in order of preference: saltskinandlaser.com, saltmedspa.com, getsalt.com, or saltclinic.com — choose based on availability, memorability, and alignment with how clients will search for and talk about the brand. Also register common misspellings and the .co, .net, or relevant alternative TLDs to prevent squatting and confusion. Use a registrar like Namecheap, Google Domains (now Squarespace Domains), or Cloudflare Registrar and enable auto-renew and domain privacy.

### Social Media Handle Reservation

Reserve your chosen handle on every major platform immediately, even those you do not plan to use at launch. This includes Instagram, TikTok, Facebook, Pinterest, LinkedIn, X (formerly Twitter), YouTube, and Threads. Consistency is ideal — the same handle everywhere. If your preferred handle is taken on one platform, document the discrepancy in your brand guide so all materials link correctly. This is a time-sensitive task — do it as soon as the name is finalized, before any public announcement.

### Brand Usage Policy

Create a document governing how external parties (partners, press, affiliates, influencers) may use the S.A.L.T. brand assets. Specify approved and prohibited uses of the logo, approved color and placement requirements, restrictions on modifying the logo, and guidelines for how the brand name should be written in text. This protects brand integrity as you grow and begin working with more external collaborators. It can be a simple one-to-two page PDF derived from the brand style guide.

### Photography & Model Release Templates

Prepare standard legal release forms for use with clients whose images (particularly before-and-after photos) will be used in marketing. The release should cover usage across all channels (print, digital, social media, advertising), be clearly written, and be presented to clients for signature during the intake process or at the time of photography. Also prepare a separate release for any models, influencers, or staff featured in brand photography. Have these reviewed by a healthcare attorney familiar with HIPAA and state medical privacy regulations, as med spa patient imagery sits at the intersection of marketing law and medical privacy.
